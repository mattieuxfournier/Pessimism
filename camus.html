Albert Camus

1913-1960

Nobel Prize-winning philosopher of the absurd and advocate of revolt

Key Aphorisms

"There is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide."

"In the midst of winter, I found there was, within me, an invincible summer."

"The only way to deal with an unfree world is to become so absolutely free that your very existence is an act of rebellion."

Philosophical Ideas

  • The absurd
  • Revolt against meaninglessness
  • Philosophical suicide vs physical suicide
  • Ethics of rebellion
  • Solidarity in the face of the absurd

Biography

Albert Camus was born in 1913 in French Algeria to a poor household. Despite battling tuberculosis, he worked his way through school and university. Camus became a journalist and participated in the French Resistance during World War II, editing the underground newspaper Combat.

He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1957 at just 44 years old, becoming the second-youngest recipient. Camus died tragically in a car accident in 1960 with an unfinished manuscript in his briefcase.

Legacy

Though he rejected the existentialist label, Camus's explorations of absurdity and meaning made him a key figure in existential thought. His novels like "The Stranger" and essays like "The Myth of Sisyphus" remain widely read and influential.

Camus's philosophy of revolt and solidarity continues to inspire political and ethical discussions. His emphasis on finding meaning through struggle rather than in metaphysical answers resonates in our increasingly secular age.
