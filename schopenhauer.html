Arthur Schopenhauer

1788-1860

German philosopher best known for his work "The World as Will and Representation"

Key Aphorisms

"Life swings like a pendulum backward and forward between pain and boredom."

"The happiness of a person is never the same as the happiness he thinks he has."

"Every man takes the limits of his own field of vision for the limits of the world."

Philosophical Ideas

  • The world as will and representation
  • Pessimism as a philosophical stance
  • Influence of Eastern philosophy on Western thought
  • The concept of the "will to live"
  • Aesthetics as a temporary escape from suffering

Biography

Arthur Schopenhauer was born in Danzig (now Gdańsk, Poland) in 1788. He studied at the University of Göttingen and later Berlin, where he developed his philosophical system in opposition to Hegel's idealism. Schopenhauer was known for his cantankerous personality and lived most of his later life in Frankfurt in relative isolation from the academic establishment.

His magnum opus, "The World as Will and Representation," was largely ignored during his early career but gained recognition later in his life. Schopenhauer's philosophy was deeply influenced by Plato, Kant, and ancient Indian philosophy, particularly Buddhism.

Legacy

Schopenhauer's work had a profound influence on later philosophers, artists, and thinkers. His philosophy directly influenced Friedrich Nietzsche, who initially admired Schopenhauer before developing his own ideas. Other notable figures influenced by Schopenhauer include Richard Wagner, Leo Tolstoy, Thomas Mann, and Sigmund Freud.

Schopenhauer's pessimistic worldview and his integration of Eastern philosophical concepts into Western philosophy marked a significant development in 19th-century thought. His emphasis on the primacy of will over intellect and his views on art and aesthetics continue to be studied and debated today.
